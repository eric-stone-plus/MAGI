# Phase 1 · GOLD (金) — Verification Audit

## Role: "Is this TRUE?"

### Verification Results

**✅ CONFIRMED — Socrates wrote nothing**
All four sources (Plato, Xenophon, Aristophanes, Aristotle) are correctly cited. No surviving texts attributed to Socrates himself. Scholarly consensus unanimous.

**✅ CONFIRMED — The Socratic Problem is real**
The contradiction between Plato's idealized Socrates, Xenophon's practical Socrates, Aristophanes' caricature, and Aristotle's second-hand reports is a genuine, unresolved scholarly problem. Reference: Vlastos 1991, "Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher."

**✅ CONFIRMED — Elenchus method description**
Five-step procedure is accurate. Key detail: Socrates does NOT claim to teach — he claims ignorance. The method is destructive (exposes false beliefs) not constructive. Reference: Vlastos, "The Socratic Elenchus" (1983).

**✅ CONFIRMED — Euthyphro 10a**
Stephanus reference correct. The dilemma is at 10a: "Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?" Unresolved — Euthyphro leaves at 15e. This is universally recognized as foundational in meta-ethics.

**⚠️ CONTESTED — Third Man Argument location**
Standard attribution: Parmenides 132a-b. However, the argument is more precisely at 132a1-b2, and its clearest formulation is actually Aristotle's (Metaphysics 990b17). The evidence package should note that Plato presents it through Parmenides (the character), not as Plato's own view — it's presented as a PROBLEM for the Theory of Forms that young Socrates cannot answer. The implication that Plato "devastates" his own theory is an overstatement — some scholars (e.g., Fine 1993) argue Plato is genuinely exploring difficulties, not repudiating Forms.

**⚠️ CONTESTED — Dialogue chronology (early/middle/late)**
The three-group classification is the STANDARD view (established by 19th-century German scholarship, confirmed by stylometry). However:
- Charles Kahn (1996, "Plato and the Socratic Dialogue") argues for a "unitarian" reading — the dialogues are literary works, not developmental stages
- Brandwood (1990) stylometric studies support the three-group division but with fuzzy boundaries
- Specific disputes: Meno is sometimes early-late, sometimes early-middle; Parmenides is sometimes middle-late, sometimes late; Phaedrus location debated
- The grouping is a useful heuristic, NOT a proven historical fact

**✅ CONFIRMED — Protagoras 345d-e: "No one does wrong willingly"**
This is the canonical Socratic paradox. The exact formulation varies: "No one willingly goes toward what they believe to be bad" is more precise. The claim that this "eliminates akrasia" is correct for Socratic intellectualism but slightly overstated — some scholars (e.g., Reshotko 2006) argue Socrates allows for non-rational desire.

**⚠️ CONTESTED — Republic 514a-520a (Allegory of the Cave)**
Stephanus references are approximately correct (the allegory spans 514a-521b). The claim that it's "not strictly Socratic but Platonic" is accurate — it appears in the middle dialogues where Socrates is Plato's mouthpiece. However, calling it merely about "appearance vs reality" undersells its political dimension: the allegory is fundamentally about EDUCATION and the philosopher's OBLIGATION to return to the cave (rule).

**✅ CONFIRMED — Phaedo immortality arguments**
Four arguments correctly identified with approximate Stephanus references:
- Opposites: 70c-72e ✓
- Recollection: 72e-77a ✓
- Affinity: 78b-84b ✓
- Form of Life: 102a-107b ✓
Simmias' harmony objection (85e-86d) and Cebes' weaver objection (86d-88c) correctly noted.

**✅ CONFIRMED — Trial of Socrates (399 BCE)**
Charges: corrupting youth (Meletus), not believing city gods (Meletus), introducing new divinities (Meletus). Anytus and Lycon as co-prosecutors. Vote: 280 guilty, 220 innocent (approximately — some sources say 281-220). Death sentence. All historically accepted.

**⚠️ CONTESTED — Popper's totalitarian critique**
Karl Popper, "The Open Society and Its Enemies" Vol. 1 (1945) — real book, real critique. However, Popper's reading of Plato is widely considered reductive by professional classicists. R.M. Hare, Julia Annas, and others have defended Plato against the totalitarian charge. The evidence package should present this as ONE interpretation, not consensus.
