## I. THE SOCRATIC PROBLEM
Socrates wrote nothing. Sources:
- Plato (primary, literary/philosophical)
- Xenophon (Memorabilia, Symposium, Apology, Oeconomicus — practical)
- Aristophanes (The Clouds — satire)
- Aristotle (second-hand, never met Socrates)

## II. SOCRATIC METHOD: THE ELENCHUS
1. Interlocutor claims knowledge
2. Socrates asks for definition
3. Definition given
4. Contradictions drawn out via questions
5. Definition refuted → aporia (impasse)

## III. SOCRATES' 7 MAJOR APORIAI

### 1. The Unity of Virtue
Are all virtues one? If knowing the good = doing the good, all virtue = knowledge.
Evidence: Protagoras argues unity; Laches/Nicias suggest partial unity; Meno 88a-89a.

### 2. Virtue and Knowledge (Socratic Intellectualism)
"No one does wrong willingly" (Protagoras 345d-e). Wrongdoing = ignorance.
Eliminates akrasia. Aristotle's critique in Protrepticus.

### 3. The Akrasia Problem
Can one act against better judgment? Socrates: NO (Protagoras 352a-358d).
Aristotle: YES (NE VII.1-11). Republic's tripartite soul explains how.

### 4. The Euthyphro Dilemma
"Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or pious because loved by gods?" (Euthyphro 10a)
Horn 1: Morality independent of God (moral realism)
Horn 2: Morality arbitrary (divine command theory)
Unresolved — Euthyphro walks away.

### 5. The Allegory of the Cave (Republic 514a-520a)
Prisoners see shadows = reality. One freed → sees sun. Returns → disbelieved.
Questions: appearance vs reality? Education? Philosophy vs politics?

### 6. The Theory of Forms
"One over Many" (Republic 596a): one Form of Bed, many particular beds.
Problems: Third Man Argument (Parmenides 132a-b), self-predication, participation, Aristotle's critique.

### 7. Immortality of the Soul (Phaedo)
Four arguments: Opposites (70c-72e), Recollection (72e-77a), Affinity (78b-84b), Form of Life (102a-107b).
Objections: Simmias (soul = harmony), Cebes (soul long-lived but not immortal).

## V. PLATO'S DIALOGUES — CHRONOLOGY

### Early (Socratic) — aporetic, no positive doctrine:
- Ion: poetic inspiration vs techne
- Laches: courage → aporia
- Charmides: temperance → aporia
- Euthyphro: piety → Euthyphro dilemma
- Protagoras: virtue teachable? virtues one?
- Gorgias: rhetoric vs philosophy; suffer or do injustice?
- Hippias Major: beauty → aporia
- Hippias Minor: voluntary vs involuntary wrongdoing
- Menexenus: parody of rhetoric
- Apology, Crito: historical/legal

### Middle (Transitional) — positive Platonic doctrine:
- Meno: virtue, anamnesis, mathematical examples
- Phaedo: immortality, Forms
- Symposium: Eros, Diotima's ladder → Form of Beauty
- Republic: ideal state, justice, Forms, Cave, Sun, Divided Line, philosopher-kings
- Phaedrus: love, rhetoric, soul as charioteer

### Late (Critical/Technical) — Plato revises own views:
- Parmenides: Third Man devastates Forms
- Theaetetus: knowledge ≠ perception, ≠ true belief, ≠ true belief with logos
- Sophist: non-being, communion of Forms
- Statesman: statesmanship, diairesis
- Timaeus: cosmology, Demiurge, khora
- Critias: Atlantis (unfinished)
- Philebus: pleasure vs intelligence
- Laws: second-best state, rule of law (no Socrates)

## IX. ARISTOTLE'S CRITIQUE OF THE REPUBLIC (天然 Myrrh)

Aristotle studied at Plato's Academy for ~20 years (367-347 BCE). His critique in **Politics Book II (ch. 1-5)** is the most systematic ancient counter-argument.

### 1. The Unity Problem (Politics 1261a)
- Plato wants the city to be "as unified as possible" — Aristotle says a city is INHERENTLY a plurality
- Extreme unity destroys the city's self-sufficiency and diversity
- A city needs farmers, artisans, warriors, rulers — unity collapses it into a household or individual
- **Core**: Plato confuses "city" with "household/person"

### 2. Common Property
- "Common things are neglected" (proto-tragedy of the commons)
- Private property + legal regulation of shared use > forced communism
- Plato's system creates TWO cities: one communist (guardians), one private (producers) — unity fails

### 3. Wives and Children in Common (most intense criticism)
- Parents who don't know their children cannot give special care
- Incest risk increases unknowingly
- Friendship (philia) requires special bonds — universal dilution weakens ALL bonds
- State-run childcare cannot replace family's natural education function
- **Result**: the opposite of what Plato intended — weaker, not stronger, social cohesion

### 4. Whose Happiness?
- The city exists for ALL citizens' eudaimonia, not just the guardian elite
- Using the majority as instruments violates the nature of a free political community
- Guardians themselves are deprived of family, property, normal emotions — are they truly happy?

### 5. The Philosopher-King Problem
- Philosopher-kings must be COMPELLED to rule — a structural defect
- The education system is too idealistic for real-world implementation
- Aristotle prefers mixed constitution (polity) with strong middle class

### Aristotle's Positive Alternative
- Private property + regulated sharing (moderate private ownership)
- Family is natural and necessary
- Best practical constitution: polity led by middle class
- Education for ALL citizens' virtue, not just elite philosopher-kings
- City = diverse, self-sufficient community pursuing the common good

### Historical Significance
- Marks the shift from Platonic rationalist idealism to Aristotelian empiricism and moderation
- Influences: Aquinas (mediating Plato/Aristotle), Locke (property rights), Montesquieu (separation of powers), Popper (open society)
