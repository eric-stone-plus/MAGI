## Gold → Frankincense Review

Gold verifies Frankincense's claim that "every freight rate was corrupted (Type A)".

**Assessment**: CONFIRMED. Gold's verification of 22 claims shows 15 REFUTED (68%), and every single freight rate number is truncated. Frankincense's three-type error taxonomy (truncation / misclassification / fabrication) accurately describes the data. The "architecture of corruption" framing is precise — these are not random errors.

**One refinement**: Frankincense suggests Excel cell width as a possible cause. Gold notes that the truncation pattern is more consistent with a CSV/fixed-width export than cell display — the numbers are systematically missing their tens digit, not showing "####" which would be the Excel behavior.

## Gold → Myrrh Review

Gold verifies Myrrh's Risk 2 (KALIORANG fabrication).

**Assessment**: CONFIRMED. Gold verified that KALIORANG has 3 COA voyages and 0 spot. Myrrh's severity rating (HIGH) is appropriate — presenting non-existent spot data to inspectors is a compliance issue regardless of intent.

**One addition**: Gold notes that the fabricated ".5-8.3" spot range for KALIORANG closely matches the actual COA range (5.87~8.35) with digits stripped. This strengthens the hypothesis that the error is a data processing pipeline failure, not intentional fabrication. But from the inspection team's perspective, intent doesn't matter — the effect is the same.

## Frankincense → Gold Review

Frankincense assesses Gold's verification methodology.

**Assessment**: Gold's 22-claim verification is thorough but could be deeper on one dimension: the COA/spot classification logic. Gold reports the discrepancy (36 vs 25 COA for ADANG BAY) but doesn't investigate WHY the classification differs. Frankincense suggests examining whether the report author used shipowner name (中远海 = COA) instead of cargo type field, which would explain the 25 count (中远海 20 + 中远海（长协）5? No, that's 28). The actual explanation remains unclear and warrants investigation.

**Additional finding**: Gold's Claim 19 analysis reveals that NEWCASTLE COA mean > spot mean, which contradicts the report's core thesis. This is a HIGH severity finding that Gold correctly identified but didn't emphasize enough.

## Frankincense → Myrrh Review

Frankincense assesses Myrrh's risk matrix.

**Assessment**: Myrrh's 8 risks are comprehensive. Two additions:

1. **Missing Risk: Organizational liability** — If the report was generated by an automated system (as Frankincense hypothesizes), the organizational failure extends beyond this one document. How many other reports submitted to regulators/inspectors have similar data corruption? Myrrh's Risk 8 (report sent back) could cascade into a broader audit of all automated reports.

2. **Risk 4 needs stronger framing** — Myrrh correctly identifies that NEWCASTLE COA > spot undermines the "以量换价" argument, but the framing should be sharper: this isn't just a data error, it's a logical contradiction. The report claims COA is systematically cheaper, but the data shows the opposite for the largest route by volume.

## Myrrh → Gold Review

Myrrh assesses Gold's verification gaps.

**Assessment**: Gold's verification is comprehensive for the 4 main routes but misses two potential issues:

1. **Discharge port ambiguity** — Gold filtered by "珠电" in discharge port. But the ledger has entries like "珠电14.2" (a discharge port that contains "珠电" but also has a number). Are these the same destination? If "珠电14.2" is a different berth, the voyage counts could be off.

2. **Cargo type field reliability** — Gold treats the "货类型" column as ground truth. But if this column is itself unreliable (e.g., manually entered, inconsistent conventions), then the COA/spot classification "correction" might be based on equally flawed data. Gold should note this assumption.

## Myrrh → Frankincense Review

Myrrh assesses Frankincense's synthesis.

**Assessment**: Frankincense's "architecture of corruption" framework is strong but has one blind spot: it assumes the errors are unintentional. Myrrh notes that the systematic nature of the errors (always truncating in a way that makes numbers smaller/more ambiguous) could also be consistent with deliberate obfuscation — making the numbers harder to verify. Frankincense should at least acknowledge this possibility, even if unintentional error is the more likely explanation.

## Cross-Review Consensus

All three Gifts agree on:
1. The report fails verification — majority of claims are REFUTED
2. The errors are systematic, not random
3. The report cannot be submitted to 巡视组 in its current form
4. The underlying business narrative (BDI drives freight, COA generally cheaper than spot) may be defensible, but the data supporting it is corrupted

Key disagreement:
- **Gold** sees the errors as data processing failures (technical)
- **Frankincense** sees them as organizational failures (process)
- **Myrrh** sees them as potential compliance risks (legal/regulatory)

These are not contradictory — they're complementary perspectives on the same problem. The full picture is: a technical data pipeline failure, unchecked by organizational review processes, creating legal/regulatory risk.
