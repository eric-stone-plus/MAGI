# MAGI Theology

> Design narrative of the MAGI protocol. Not architectural constraint — conceptual grammar.

## The Three Wise Men

**MAGI** (マギ / 東方三博士) takes its name from the Magi of Matthew 2:1-12 — the Wise Men from the East who followed a star to Bethlehem, bearing three gifts for the newborn King.

The protocol's three delegates are not merely named after the Gifts — they embody the theological meanings that have accumulated over two millennia of interpretation:

## The Three Gifts (Matthew 2:11)

### Gold — for a King

Gold was the gift for royalty. Incorruptible, the foundation of all value — the metal that does not tarnish, the standard against which all other wealth is measured.

In MAGI, Gold is the **factual verifier**. Its question: *is this claim correct?* Like the gift it bears, Gold's findings are meant to be incorruptible — the foundation upon which Frankincense and Myrrh build their analysis. A fact verified by Gold carries the weight of something that has been tested and found true.

Theological tradition holds that Gold prefigures Christ's kingship. In the protocol, Gold's verification prefigures the convergence: without verified facts, there can be no meaningful agreement.

### Frankincense — for a Priest

Frankincense was the gift for a priest — the incense that rises in the Temple, transforming raw material into sacred offering. It is the gift of synthesis, of seeing meaning beyond the material.

In MAGI, Frankincense is the **contextual reasoner**. Its question: *does this hold from another angle?* Frankincense does not merely verify — it rises above the facts to ask what they mean in context. A claim may be factually correct but contextually misleading; Frankincense catches this.

Theological tradition holds that Frankincense prefigures Christ's priesthood — the mediator between human and divine. In the protocol, Frankincense mediates between bare facts and contextual understanding.

### Myrrh — for One Who Would Suffer and Die

Myrrh was the strangest gift — an embalming spice, given to an infant. It is the gift of mortality, of the awareness that all things end, all structures fail, all conclusions are provisional.

In MAGI, Myrrh is the **adversarial auditor**. Its question: *what breaks if this is wrong?* Myrrh does not seek consensus — it seeks fragility. It asks the question no one wants to hear: what is the worst plausible misreading? Where does the reasoning chain fail under pressure?

Theological tradition holds that Myrrh prefigures Christ's death — the necessary confrontation with mortality that precedes resurrection. In the protocol, Myrrh's adversarial audit is the necessary confrontation with fragility that precedes confident action.

## The Trinity: Three Persons, One Substance

The three delegates are not three separate analyses — they are three persons of one inquiry. Gold, Frankincense, and Myrrh are distinct but inseparable:

- You cannot have truth without meaning (Gold needs Frankincense)
- You cannot have meaning without truth (Frankincense needs Gold)
- You cannot have either without awareness of fragility (both need Myrrh)

This mirrors the theological doctrine of the Trinity: three persons, one substance. The protocol does not produce three competing reports — it produces one converged judgment that contains three perspectives.

## The Star and the Journey

*"We have seen his star in the east, and are come to worship him."* — Matthew 2:2

The Magi did not know where they were going. They followed a star whose meaning they could not fully understand, across terrain they had never crossed, toward a destination they could not name.

MAGI's hm-triggered dispatch echoes this: hm dispatches the delegates not because hm knows the answer, but because hm knows it does not know. The delegates are sent forth with a question and return with testimony. The convergence gate is the manger — the point where three independent journeys meet.

## Design Narrative, Not Architectural Constraint

The theological framework enriches MAGI's conceptual grammar but generates no testable predictions beyond what the cited ML literature provides. No design decision is derived from theology — the protocol would function identically with different names. The framework is classified as "design narrative" following the QUINTE v3.2 precedent on Polybius.

It exists because naming matters. A protocol called "3-Model Parallel Pre-Verification with Binary Convergence Gate" would be technically accurate and spiritually dead. The Magi followed a star. So do we.
